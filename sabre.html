What Is Sabre?

Sabre is one of the three Olympic fencing weapons and is known for its incredible speed and aggressive style. Derived from cavalry swordsmanship, sabre allows both cutting and thrusting movements, making it the most fast-paced and dynamic fencing discipline.

Only the upper body — everything above the waist except the hands — is a valid target. Sabre uses the right-of-way rule, encouraging structured offensive and defensive actions.

Rules of Sabre

  • Right-of-Way: As in foil, priority determines which fencer is awarded the point when simultaneous hits occur.
  • Target Area: Everything above the waist except the hands — torso, arms, mask.
  • Scoring: Points can be made with both the edge and the tip of the blade.
  • Bout Format: Standard competitive bouts are three 3-minute periods or until one fencer reaches 15 touches.
  • Bladework: Blocks (parries), cuts, and quick transitions dominate sabre strategy.
  • Equipment: A metallic lamé jacket and conductive mask cover the target area for electric scoring.
  • Speed of Action: Exchanges are extremely fast; the referee stops action upon a scoring light or halt call.
